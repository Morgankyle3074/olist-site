Research Paper

Methodology, engineered metrics, and findings for the Olist marketplace dataset.

Business Problem

Marketplace e-commerce fulfillment is a chained system: approval workflows, seller processing, carrier handoff, and last-mile delivery. Disruption at any point can cascade into customer-visible delays.

The core question is stage-level: Which part of the fulfillment pipeline contributes most to late deliveries? Instead of looking at isolated orders, this project treats fulfillment as a system and measures how variability propagates through the pipeline to create late outcomes. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Charts & Visual Findings

Bottleneck signals from last-mile time, order complexity, and delay distribution.

Order Complexity vs Fulfillment Time

Items per order shows limited explanatory power for delay behavior.

The relationship between items per order and total cycle time is weak, indicating complexity is not a primary delay driver. Variability concentrates among low-complexity orders—challenging the assumption that “bigger orders = later deliveries.” :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Delivery Delay Distribution

Early-fulfillment bias with tail-risk late exceptions.

Deliveries skew early/on-time overall, but a minority of orders experience extreme lateness. Operationally, the delay story is driven by tail events rather than uniform underperformance. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Last-Mile Duration vs Delivery Delay

A direct relationship: longer last-mile transit corresponds to larger delays.

The upward pattern shows delay severity increases with last-mile duration. As last-mile time grows, variability widens too, reflecting rising volatility and risk accumulation in extended delivery networks. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Project Thought Process

This project reframes fulfillment as a sequence of measurable stages. Raw event timestamps were transformed into operational metrics (approval time, seller processing duration, last-mile time, total cycle time, and delivery delay) to support bottleneck diagnosis and delay propagation analysis. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

  • Goal: Identify which fulfillment stage dominates delay creation.
  • Method: Stage-level metric engineering (SQL) + visual diagnostics (Tableau).
  • Primary bottleneck: Last-mile delivery dominates both cycle time and delay severity. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}
  • Secondary insight: Order complexity is not a strong predictor of delays. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Systems-Level Lens

Instead of treating late deliveries as random, the analysis tests where variability concentrates across the pipeline, and whether late outcomes trace back to a dominant stage.

Dominant Driver

Last-mile duration is the strongest contributor to total fulfillment time and is structurally linked to late delivery outcomes, making it the highest-leverage optimization target. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Delay Behavior

The network shows early-delivery clustering alongside volatile late exceptions—classic “tail-risk” delay behavior, consistent with real-world logistics volatility. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Actionable Direction

Interventions focused on last-mile optimization and regional segmentation offer higher expected impact than upstream tweaks to approval or seller processing. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Operational Takeaway: If you improve one thing first, improve the last mile—because that’s where cycle time expands and where lateness scales.
